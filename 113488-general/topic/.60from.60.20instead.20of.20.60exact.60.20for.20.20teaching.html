Topic: `from` instead of `exact` for teaching

{% raw %}

Gihan Marasingha (Aug 07 2021 at 14:44):

While re-writing my lecture notes for my introductory proof course, I realised that no mathematician says, 'this follows by exact h', but we often say, 'this follows from h'.

As the from tactic is synonymous to exact, I plan to us from everywhere. The only downside is that students might be confused by exact if they read mathlib. Any thoughts? Is this a good plan or a bad plan?

I'm also veering more to teaching inductive proofs by pattern matching rather than the induction tactic. Pattern matching does a cleaner job of announcing the cases and avoids needing to name the induction hypothesis. In this, it's closer to a tradition mathematical proof.

Eric Wieser (Aug 07 2021 at 17:31):

Are you aware of tactic#case, which allows you to announce the cases for induction?

Eric Wieser (Aug 07 2021 at 17:32):

Using pattern matching alone doesn't work very well if a proof starts the induction halfway through

Gihan Marasingha (Aug 07 2021 at 22:50):

Thanks. I understand the deficiencies of proofs by pattern-matching, especially in Lean 3 and I will still be teaching the induction tactic for that very reason. Nonetheless, I feel that for students who have just encountered induction, a pattern-matching proof may be easier to understand. Of course, only experience will determine the truth of the matter.

My arguments are two fold. First, a proof by pattern matching looks like (indeed is) a recursive construction. If students are taught recursion via pattern matching, then they may be more likely to understand induction as merely another example of recursion.

Second was my point about the clarity of the argument, especially when the proof is presented non-interactively.

To take an example, consider proving 0+n=n0 + n = n0+n=n, for every natural number nnn. An informal proof might be:

Write P(n)P(n)P(n) for the statement 0+n=n0 + n = n0+n=n. We prove ∀n,P(n)\forall n, P(n)∀n,P(n) by induction.
Base case: P(0)P(0)P(0) is the assertion 0+0=00 + 0 = 00+0=0, which follows by reflexivity.
Inductive step: we must show P(k+1)P(k + 1)P(k+1), i.e. $$0 + (k + 1) = k + 1$ on the assumption P(k)P(k)P(k), i.e. 0+k=k0 + k = k0+k=k. Rewriting with the
definition of addition, we must show (0+k)+1=k+1(0 + k) + 1 = k + 1(0+k)+1=k+1. Rewriting with P(k)P(k)P(k), this is k+1=k+1k + 1 = k + 1k+1=k+1.

This translates fairly directly into the following pattern-matching proof. The major difference here is that I'm not exclaiming, "here's a proof by induction". I'm also using zero_add n in place of P(n)P(n)P(n).

lemma zero_add : ∀ (n : ℕ), 0 + n = n
  | 0       := rfl
  | (k + 1) := by rw [nat.add_succ, zero_add k]

A proof using the induction tactic has the advantage that it does, at least, use the word induction. Other than that, it doesn't look much like the hand-written proof. The names of the cases are the names of the constructors of nat, which is not likely to be how a new maths undergraduate thinks of induction. It's not so clear what ih refers to in the succ case, whereas the reference to zero_add k in the pattern-matching proof is quite explicit.

lemma zero_add' (n : ℕ) : 0 + n = n :=
begin
  induction n,
  case zero { refl },
  case succ : k ih { rw [nat.add_succ, ih] }
end
