Kyle Miller (Jul 30 2022 at 00:25):

Last quarter I generated a review sheet for my combinatorics course with openAI, and as part of our midterm review session we went through it to figure out how it was wrong and how to fix it.

Some favorites:

Example. A group of 10 people are sitting around a table. Show that there must be two people sitting next to each other who have the same birthday.

Solution. There are 10 people and 365 days in a year, so by the pigeonhole principle, two people must have the same birthday.

Example. There are 12 eggs in a carton. Show that there must be two eggs with the same weight.

Solution. There are 12 eggs and there are only so many different weights that eggs can have, so by the pigeonhole principle, two eggs must have the same weight.

Jason Rute (Jul 30 2022 at 00:27):

@Heather Macbeth
This prompt probably works better and is a bit more consistent for me.

Theorem: The sum of two odd numbers is even.
Proof.

If you prompt with 

Prove that the sum of two odd numbers is even.

it might just repeat the question with different versions, like it is given a homework problem sheet. Here is the response from Codex, which has a bit more math in it's training than GPT-3, but GPT-3 also gave me the same basic proof.

Theorem: The sum of two odd numbers is even.
Proof. Let $a$ and $b$ be odd numbers. Then $a = 2k + 1$ and $b = 2l + 1$ for some integers $k$ and $l$. Then $a + b = 2k + 1 + 2l + 1 = 2(k + l) + 2 = 2(k + l + 1)$, which is even.
