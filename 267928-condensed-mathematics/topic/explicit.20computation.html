Note that there's a (family of) involutions Fn:□n→□nF_n : \square^n \to \square^nFn​:□n→□n which I'll call bit-flip maps, defined by

(x1,…,xn)↦(1−x1,…,1−xn)(x_1, \ldots, x_n) \mapsto (1-x_1, \ldots, 1-x_n)(x1​,…,xn​)↦(1−x1​,…,1−xn​),

Using all the jargon: The linearization of the functor A↦Z[A]A\mapsto \mathbb Z[A]A↦Z[A] is the functor A↦Z⊗SAA\mapsto \mathbb Z\otimes_{\mathbb S} AA↦Z⊗S​A (this is an easy exercise in the definition of "linearization" = "Goodwillie derivative"). This means that the linearization of C(A)C(A)C(A) can be computed by a complex of the form

C(A)lin:…→Z⊗SA4→Z⊗SA2→Z⊗SA→0,C(A)^{\mathrm{lin}}: \ldots \to \mathbb Z\otimes_{\mathbb S} A^4\to \mathbb Z\otimes_{\mathbb S} A^2\to \mathbb Z\otimes_{\mathbb S} A\to 0,C(A)lin:…→Z⊗S​A4→Z⊗S​A2→Z⊗S​A→0,

where one has to suitably interpret this (as each term is a complex itself, so we're really doing some double complex stuff). But C(A)C(A)C(A) is already linear in AAA, so this complex is quasi-isomorphic to C(A)C(A)C(A).

by telescoping.
