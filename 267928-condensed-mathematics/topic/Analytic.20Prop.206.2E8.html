So we now have a clear understanding of the functor. The next thing to check is that it takes values in comm_rings. For both addition and multiplication this boils down to the assertion that if ∑n∈ZanTn\sum_{n\in\mathbb{Z}}a_nT^n∑n∈Z​an​Tn and ∑n∈ZbnTn\sum_{n\in\mathbb{Z}}b_nT^n∑n∈Z​bn​Tn are in Z[[T]][1/T]\mathbb{Z}[[T]][1/T]Z[[T]][1/T] (i.e., have at worst poles at 0) and both series converge absolutely at T=rT=rT=r then so does their sum, difference and product (note r>0r>0r>0 so there's no problem with poles). This is standard. Note of course that the bounds can change -- Z((T))r,≤c\mathbb{Z}((T))_{r,\leq c}Z((T))r,≤c​ isn't a condensed ring, it's like the reals (a ring) being the union of [−c,c][-c,c][−c,c] (not rings) as cc grows.

The next claim is that Z((T))r,≤c\mathbb{Z}((T))_{r,\leq c}Z((T))r,≤c​ and Z((T))r\mathbb{Z}((T))_rZ((T))r​ are sheaves. Results from Condensed.pdf tell us that to check something is a sheaf we just have to check that it sends finite disjoint unions to products, which is completely formal (one only needs to check the case of unions of 0 and 2 things for the usual reasons, but arbitrary finite unions works fine), and that it plays well with surjections, which turns out to be a straightforward calculation: if S′→SS'\to SS′→S is a continuous surjection of profinite sets then it's obvious that to give a Z((T))\mathbb{Z}((T))Z((T))-valued function on SSS is to give a Z((T))\mathbb{Z}((T))Z((T))-valued function on S′S'S′ which is constant on pre-images of elements of SSS, and then the only things left to check are that bounds match up and that local constancy of the coefficients matches up.

OK so finally onto 6.8! The claim is that a condensed set "is" a profinite set. More precisely what we're going to do is to put a topology on X:=Z((T))r,≤c({∗}),X:=\mathbb{Z}((T))_{r,\leq c}(\{*\}),X:=Z((T))r,≤c​({∗}), show that it's profinite, and then prove that there's a "canonical" isomorphism from Z((T))r,≤c(S)\mathbb{Z}((T))_{r,\leq c}(S)Z((T))r,≤c​(S) to Cont(S,X),Cont(S,X),Cont(S,X), where "canonical" means "a bunch of diagrams commute"; more precisely we give an isomorphism between Z((T))r,≤c\mathbb{Z}((T))_{r,\leq c}Z((T))r,≤c​ and a sheaf represented by a profinite set. I will now proceed to not check any of the diagrams commute and just write down the isomorphism, because I am still a mathematician at heart and this post is already long enough. Everything is trivial though, one just works in the space of functions from SSS to Z((T))\mathbb{Z}((T))Z((T)) to check diagrams commute (i.e. use ext).

So XXX is just the power series ∑n∈ZanTn\sum_{n\in\mathbb{Z}}a_nT^n∑n∈Z​an​Tn with ∑n∣an∣rn≤c.\sum_n|a_n|r^n\leq c.∑n​∣an​∣rn≤c. If BBB (a negative integer) satisfies rB>cr^B>crB>c then an=0a_n=0an​=0 for n<B;n<B;n<B; more generally for a fixed nnn we have ∣an∣rn≤c|a_n|r^n\leq c∣an​∣rn≤c so there are only finitely many choices for each ana_nan​ coming from an element of XXX; this shows that for any NNN the image XNX_NXN​ of XXX under the "truncation" map sending TnT^nTn to 000 if n≥Nn\geq Nn≥N is finite, and XXX is easily checked to be a projective limit of these maps, so it inherits a profinite topology.

Given any map S→XS\to XS→X with this topology we certainly get a power series attached to each element of SSS; the only remaining thing to do (to define the map -- I'm skipping all the proofs that the diagrams commute because they're obvious) is to check that this map is continuous iff all the induced maps an:S→Za_n:S\to\mathbb{Z}an​:S→Z are locally constant. If S→XS\to XS→X is continuous then, by definition of the topology on XXX, the induced "take coefficient of TnT^nTn" map is a continuous map S→ZS\to\mathbb{Z}S→Z (the latter having the discrete topology), and is thus locally constant. Conversely if all the "coefficient of TnT^nTn" maps are locally constant, then note that this does not necessarily imply that the map S→XS\to XS→X is locally constant (a counterexample with S=ZpS=\mathbb{Z}_pS=Zp​ sends pnup^nupnu to TnT^nTn and 000 to 000) but it does imply that all the induced maps S→XNS\to X_NS→XN​ are locally constant (there are only finitely many coefficients involved, namely those between BBB and NNN) and hence continuous, so the map S→XS\to XS→X coming from the fact that XXX is the limit of the XNX_NXN​ is continuous.

I guess in retrospect what I have written here is what Patrick or Sander would call a roadmap for the proof (which will be straightforward to do in Lean).
